[CHAPTER: Why fast cars feel slow]

[thoughtful] So here's the thing about modern sports cars. The numbers on paper keep climbing — three hundred horsepower, four, five — and yet the cars feel, somehow, less alive than they did twenty years ago. [pauses]

Part of it is mass. A 2005 Boxster weighs about 2,900 pounds. The 2025 equivalent? Closer to 3,400. [exhales] That's an extra refrigerator riding shotgun. The engine has to drag it. The brakes have to stop it. The steering has to point it. Every input gets filtered through that extra weight, and the car loses the snap.

[curious] But there's a stranger thing happening underneath. Modern cars are isolated. The driver sits inside what's basically a soundproof booth on wheels — laminated glass, foam in the pillars, active noise cancellation. [chuckles] Engineers spent decades making cars quieter, and they succeeded so completely that the cars stopped feeling fast.

[pauses] Speed without sensation is just math.
