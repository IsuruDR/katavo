[CHAPTER: Why fast cars feel slow]

[thoughtful] The numbers on paper keep climbing. Three hundred horsepower, four, five. A 2025 Porsche 911 has roughly twice the output of one from 2005. And yet the cars feel, by every honest report from people who actually drove both, less alive than they used to.

Part of it is mass. A 2005 Boxster came in at about 2,900 pounds. The 2025 equivalent is closer to 3,400, depending on options — that's an extra refrigerator riding shotgun. The engine has to drag it. The brakes have to stop it. The steering has to point it. Every input gets filtered through that weight before the car responds, and the snap that used to make sports cars feel like extensions of the driver gets smoothed out.

[curious] But there's a stranger thing happening underneath. Modern cars are isolated. The driver sits inside what's effectively a soundproof booth on wheels — laminated glass, foam packed into the pillars, active noise cancellation piped through the speakers. Engineers spent thirty years making cars quieter, and they succeeded so completely that the cars stopped feeling fast. The sensation of speed isn't actually speed; it's the wind, the engine note, the slight harshness of a road surface coming through the seat. Take those away and 80 miles per hour feels like 40.

[pauses] Speed without sensation is just math.
